§1 — How to read the candidate map

The validation map shows a single ranked candidate radar feature and its spatial relationship to the expected balloon region. Here is what each element means:

Blue shaded square — Maidenhead grid-square region from balloon telemetry. This is an approximate expected balloon region derived from the telemetry grid locator, not from exact GPS coordinates. The true balloon position could be anywhere within this square.
Black circle — Expected grid-square center. The geometric center of the Maidenhead grid square. Used as the reference point for distance calculations, but the actual balloon position within the grid square is unknown.
Red marker — Candidate radar feature center. The location of the candidate radar return. This is a radar feature that scored well in the candidate search – it has not been confirmed as the balloon.
Red line — Horizontal separation. The great-circle distance between the expected grid-square center and the candidate radar feature. Shorter distances are more interesting but do not prove association.
White/gray dots — Nearby NEXRAD radar gates. Individual radar sample volumes (gates) from the same scan, colored by reflectivity. These show the local radar context around the candidate feature.
Blue highlighted points — Candidate-related gates. Gates that are part of or near the candidate cluster. These are highlighted for review context – they are not confirmed balloon detections.

Key metrics shown on the map

  • max dBZ: Strongest reflectivity in the local candidate group. Picoballoons are weak radar targets, so values near or below 0 dBZ are expected if present at all.
  • Altitude mismatch: Difference between the expected balloon altitude (from telemetry) and the radar gate/beam-center altitude. Beam-center altitude is an approximation – the actual beam has width that increases with range.
⚠ Important caveat: The balloon position is estimated from Maidenhead grid-square centers, not exact GPS. The grid square is roughly 5–9 km across. Distance and altitude figures use this estimated center, so the true separation could be larger or smaller.

§4 — Doppler intuition preview

NEXRAD measures radial velocity — the component of motion directly toward or away from the radar, not the full speed of the object. Understanding this is crucial for interpreting Doppler evidence.

Key Doppler concepts

  • Radial velocity is motion along the radar's line of sight — toward or away from the radar antenna.
  • If a balloon moves mostly sideways relative to the radar (perpendicular to the line of sight), its radial velocity can be near zero even though it is moving quickly.
  • Low radial velocity does not mean the object is stationary. It may simply mean the motion is mostly sideways.
  • Near-zero Doppler can make velocity evidence weak or harder to interpret for balloon-like targets.

Common ways to fool yourself with radar

When reviewing radar candidates for possible balloon association, be aware of these common interpretation pitfalls:

  1. Close horizontally, wrong altitude. A radar feature can appear at the same latitude/longitude as the expected balloon but at a completely different altitude. Always check both horizontal and vertical proximity.
  2. Beam width increases with range. At short range (e.g., 20 km), the NEXRAD beam is narrow (~350 m). At long range (e.g., 200 km), it is much wider (~3,500 m). This means altitude estimates from a single gate become less precise at greater distances from the radar.
  3. Radar scans are not instantaneous. A single volume scan takes several minutes. The balloon telemetry timestamp and the radar scan time may not perfectly overlap, and the balloon moves during this interval. Small timing mismatches can introduce position errors.
  4. Radial velocity is not full speed. NEXRAD Doppler measures only the toward/away component of motion. If the object moves mostly sideways relative to the radar, observed radial velocity will be small regardless of actual speed.
  5. Near-zero Doppler ≠ stationary object. A target moving perpendicular to the radar line of sight has near-zero radial velocity. Do not conclude that an object with low Doppler is not moving.
  6. Weak point targets have many possible explanations. A radar return near the expected balloon region could be weather, biological scatter (birds, insects), ground clutter, anomalous propagation, side-lobe contamination, or processing artifacts. The candidate scoring helps prioritize, but does not rule out alternative explanations.
  7. Cross-radar consistency is stronger, but still not proof. Seeing similar features from multiple radars at the same time and location significantly increases confidence, but coincidental agreement is possible. Multi-radar association should be treated as strong evidence, not definitive proof.
